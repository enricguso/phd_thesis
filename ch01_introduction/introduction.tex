\subsection{Dealing with the domain mismatch}

When working with different music datasets–-e.g. recordings with distinct instrument timbres, repertoires or styles, audio quality, effects, and many more–-we generally say that we have different \textit{domains}. A \acrshort{DL} model trained on one domain often shows suboptimal performance when tested on another. This problem arises because the data distributions differ, a phenomenon that is known as dataset or domain shift~\citep{quinonero-dataset_shift}. 


Let $X$ be a set of inputs from a feature space $\mathcal{X}$. A \textit{domain} is defined as $\mathcal{D} = (\mathcal{X}, P(X))$, being $P(X)$ the marginal probability distribution. Then, a \textit{task} is defined by labels $Y$ (from label space $\mathcal{Y}$) and the conditional distribution $P(Y|X)$) such that $\mathcal{T} = (\mathcal{Y},  P(Y|X))$. In supervised learning, $P(Y|X)$ is the function that is learned. The problem of domain shift appears when applying, for the same task $\mathcal{T}$, a model trained on a given domain on a different one. 
%
\textit{Domain adaptation} methods aim to bridge this gap.\footnote{When tasks also differ, we may employ techniques to leverage the knowledge learnt for one task to address a different one. This is known as \textit{transfer learning}~\citep{pan-transfer}.}


The general idea of domain adaptation is to train the model using a large, labeled dataset from source domain $\mathcal{D}_S$, and then adapt the model using a smaller dataset from the target domain $\mathcal{D}_T$, often with few or no labels, aiming at the system to generalize effectively to the target domain, despite the distributional difference~\citep{redko-domain}. While supervised or semi-supervised domain adaptation assume some labeled examples in $\mathcal{D}_T$~\citep{kouw-domain,farahani-domain}, unsupervised domain adaptation uses labeled data from $\mathcal{D}_S$ and completely unlabeled data from $\mathcal{D}_T$.  


Given the vast and diverse world of music, there is an important body of literature for domain adaptation in the \acrshort{MIR} field, generally aiming at transferring knowledge to domains for which annotated and clean data is scarce or prohibitively expensive to collect. Let us take a look at a few relevant domain works for the tasks studied in this thesis.

\subsection{Domain shift in melody estimation}

In the context of estimating the main or multiple melodies in music signals, domain adaptation has been carried out, for instance, to leverage knowledge from the speech domain to the singing voice~\citep{morrison-cross_domain}, to adapt to different microphone qualities and characteristics~\citep{bittner-multi_microphone}, or to leverage piano transcription knowledge to address the same task, in a symbolic fashion, for another polyphonic instrument~\citep{riley-domain_adaptation}, showing that a domain mismatch exist in all these cases.

%\citep{morrison-cross_domain} cross domain pitch estimation adapting from the speech domain, where more data is available, to speech. The system is working on monophonic signals and mostly on the same language. \citep{bittner-multi_microphone} performs domain adaptation between microphones, and \citep{riley-domain_adaptation} leverages piano transcription knowledge to attempt symbolic guitar transcription. 

Interestingly, the problem of domain shift for \acrshort{VME} from polyphonic music signals is empirically shown in \citep{saxena-interactive_extraction}, including a Hindustani music experiment in the analysis.
%
%
To alleviate the domain gap, the authors propose a method integrating active- and meta-learning techniques to select the least confident preliminarily predicted melody values and show them to a human annotator, who refines the prediction. Using meta-learning, the model adapts to the improved predictions.
%
The paper shows performance boost to diverse datasets, one being a Hindustani vocal melody dataset created for the purpose of the work.\footnote{\url{https://zenodo.org/records/8252222}}
%
However, the instruments in this dataset are synthetic, and only two different singers are recorded using the same setup, therefore it is not clear if the evaluation results extrapolate to real recordings. Also, the pitch annotations in the proposed dataset are automatically extracted from the solo singer stem with no post-processing or refinement considered, and therefore it is subject to the estimation errors. As a final remark, the proposed system necessitates involvement of human annotators, which is a costly and time-consuming process.


\subsection{Domain shift in music source separation}

The problem of \acrshort{MSS} is fundamentally domain-dependent, with performance hinging on characteristics such as timbre, instrumentation, and recording conventions that vary greatly across music traditions~\citep{jeon-cacophony,sanidha-multimodal}. Convolutional and spectral-domain \acrshort{DL} models in particular must learn timbre-specific features to perform effectively~\citep{pons-timbre}, which makes instrumentation and performance practices a crucial aspect.
%
Existing work highlights how even production-level processes, such as mastering and loudness leveling, can cause significant performance degradation when open models in the literature are used on real-world mixtures~\citep{jeon-loud}. 
%For instance, Jeon Lee (2022) show that systems trained under standard conditions degrade substantially on datasets modified to reflect commercial loudness compression (e.g., musdb‑L/XL), and require specialized augmentation (LimitAug) to restore robustness.

Beyond data augmentations, domain adaptation has also been explored to address timbre and dataset-specific mismatches. One-shot adaptation methods allow quick fine-tuning of pre-trained models on a single mixture, using user-provided activity labels to improve separation for unrepresented instruments~\citep{cantisani-separation_domain}.
%
%
%Transfer learning across datasets offers marginal benefits: Bugler et al. (2020) find that models pretrained on synthetic instrument mixes only modestly improve when adapted to real-world splits—suggesting limited cross-domain transferability~\citep{bugler-transfer_separation}.
%
A particularly revealing case is harmonic-percussive separation (HPSS): \citep{lordelo-HPSS} apply adversarial domain adaptation to shift an HPSS model trained using Western commercial recordings to Scandinavian fiddle and foot-tapping recordings. Their results show clear gains, confirming that even subsets of music with strong domain consistency demand adaptation.
%
Interestingly, prior efforts have attempted domain adaptation for MSS~\citep{bugler-transfer_separation} showing modest improvement for small synthetic instrumental datasets. This indicates the particular complexity of generalization of the separation problem in music, which is strongly conditioned by the difficulty of collecting real, clean, multi-stem tracks.


Other attempts consider one and few-shot adaptation methods to unseen instruments~\citep{wang-source_fewshot}, or stem-agnostic systems to query a more fine-grained set of music sources using an embedding space~\citep{watcharasupat-agnostic}. These approaches, despite still limited in performance, show promising results and highlight the relevance of separation systems accounting for instrument setups and domains that go beyond Western commercial music recordings and instrumentation. %, while showing the potential of \acrshort{DL} to adapt to new domains.




\subsection{The Carnatic use case: (a lot of) live, multi-stem data}
Given its cultural traits, Carnatic music presents a particularly interesting use case; it is highly improvisational and requires live interaction between performing artists on stage. Carnatic performances are mostly enjoyed live~\citep{sarala-applause,srinivasamurthy-saraga}, and digital distribution is generally based on recordings from live performances, or in some cases, on studio recordings having all artists playing in the same room~\citep{srinivasamurthy-saraga}. Therefore, it is uncommon to record a Carnatic performance as done in modern productions: recording each stem separately in the studio, which is actually the ideal setup to collect data for developing \acrshort{MSS} systems. Recording artists simultaneously but completely isolated one from the other requires a complex setup, involving live visual streaming if inter-artist interaction wants to be enabled~\citep{sanidha-multimodal}. This may potentially put artists in an unnatural, uncomfortable situation.
%
Even in this context, it is difficult to collect large datasets with representative diversity and domain realism.


However, multi-stem recordings are crucial for the development of \acrshort{DL} systems for vocal analysis~\citep{cuesta-mir_web,schwar-larynx}, melody estimation~\citep{bittner-medleydb,salamon-analysis_synthesis,jeon-medleyvox}, and separation~\citep{hsu-mir1k,rafii-musdb}.
Interestingly, we can find large and diverse multi-stem recording collections of real live concerts for Carnatic music in the literature~\citep{srinivasamurthy-saraga}.\footnote{During the development of this thesis, we have contributed to the publication of another large collection of multi-stem recordings for Carnatic music, in this case including video and gesture features~\citep{shankar-saraga_audiovisual}. More details are found in Chapter~\ref{ch03_data}, Section~\ref{ch03_data:saraga_av}.} However, despite their great value, this implies an important challenge: in a music style in which all instruments are generally acoustic and amplified using a microphone, the audio signals corresponding to individual sources have \textit{source bleeding}, meaning that other sources, room acoustics, and other unwanted components are leaked in.

\section{MusicalAI}
Strongly motivated by the impact of the CompMusic project and the need for continuing the repertoire-aware research keeping on the track with the latest technological advancements, a second research initiative to support corpus-based computational analysis studies launched in the MTG in 2020. MusicalAI was a project funded by the Ministry of Science and Innovation of the Spanish Government and the Agencia Estatal de Investigación (AEI), with reference: PID2019-111403GB-I00, which spanned from 2020 to 2024. It covered four different work packages with a common underlying goal: develop \acrshort{AI} tools on large corpora in order to enhance the understanding of music. 


This thesis is part of a work package within MusicalAI that builds on the premise that a carefully constructed music corpus comprising audio recordings and rich metadata can effectively capture the essence of the musical styles. Among the various elements that describe a musical style, a group of works, or an individual piece, melodic motives serve as recurring patterns that help represent stylistic identity. Based on this idea, the project was meant to operate on the music corpora compiled during the CompMusic project, and explore methods for discovering style specific melodic patterns across them.


However, this research objective is inherently restricted by the need for high-quality audio data and descriptors that enable a more robust performance of computational melodic analysis methods. This thesis is an attempt to leverage the available Carnatic datasets and data collection practices, and to emphasize the repertoire characteristics, to develop \acrshort{DL} systems that can contribute to the correct implementation of the research goals of MusicalAI.

Nevertheless, we expect that the work in this thesis will have a broader impact, providing open systems for the research community.

\section{Research objectives}
Having the motivation and scope at hand, the main objective of this thesis is \textbf{to enhance the computational melodic analysis of Carnatic music by tailoring \acrshort{DL} processing and retrieval approaches to this style.} By taking into account the inherent characteristics, performance practices, and the nature of the openly available datasets for this repertoire, we aim at developing tailored techniques to improve on \textbf{(i)} predicting the vocal melody, and \textbf{(ii)} separating the vocal source from Carnatic music mixtures.


Next, we outline with more granularity the detailed research objectives (\textbf{RO}) we address in this dissertation:

\begin{itemize}[align=left,leftmargin=*,labelsep=0.5em]
    \item[\textbf{RO-1} $\rightarrow$] \textbf{Design techniques to leverage multi-stem live recordings for the development of \acrshort{DL} approaches.} Multi-stem audio, which is relevant for many \acrshort{MIR} applications, is significantly less expensive and time-consuming to collect in live performances than fully-isolated in the studio. However, this carries particular challenges mostly given the presence of interfering signals in the solo tracks, which hinders the direct use of these recordings for \acrshort{DL} applications. There is a research interest on exploiting these recordings, which yield ecological value, inherent knowledge of the repertoire, and enables feasible data gathering at scale.
    \item[\textbf{RO-2} $\rightarrow$] \textbf{Achieve state-of-the-art performance for vocal melody estimation for Carnatic music.} Accurate and fine-grained melody contours are crucial for a more comprehensive analysis of the Carnatic melodic framework~\citep{pearson_annotating_2023}. In a literature survey we observe that researchers mostly rely on fully rule-based methods which may potentially present generalization and scalability issues~\citep{tzanetakis-genre,schedl-mir}. We aim at achieving improved melody estimation performance for Carnatic music leveraging recent \acrshort{DL}-based advancements.
    \item[\textbf{RO-3} $\rightarrow$]  \textbf{Improve on existing separation systems for Carnatic accompaniment removal.} Open, pre-trained \acrshort{SVE} systems in the literature are generally trained using fully-isolated multi-stem datasets that do not include examples from the Carnatic domain.
    %
    Consequently, these systems are particularly not able to disentangle the Carnatic accompaniment from the lead singer~\citep{sanidha-multimodal,shankar-disentangling}. We target \acrshort{SVE} for Carnatic music focusing on a more prominent accompaniment suppression, aiming at providing a cleaner vocal signal for further processing or feature retrieval, or even for direct analysis. We aim to design bleeding-aware techniques to leverage the inherent domain knowledge in live, multi-stem recordings. These are easier to compile, while for several repertoires, fully-isolated multi-stem recording procedures may hinder the ecological validity.
    \item[\textbf{RO-4} $\rightarrow$]  \textbf{Explore the potential of recent advancements on generative modeling for vocal separation.} Recent advances on generative modeling are paving the way for the development of powerful generative systems that are capable of addressing inverse tasks~\citep{serra-universe}. This dissertation examines the potential of \acrfull{DDPM}–-a novel class of powerful and flexible generative systems~\citep{ho-diffusion}–-to separate the singing voice from music mixtures. A primary objective is to empirically validate the capability of \acrshort{DDPM} to generate plausible solo vocals using the corresponding mixture as conditioner or guidance. Existing work has shown promising separation performance for instrumental music~\citep{mariani-msdm,karchkhadze-msdm_ldm,chae-mge}, however, separating the singing voice remains limited. A subsequent goal is to rely on the inherent flexibility of these systems to account for the challenges in the openly available Carnatic multi-stem data, prioritizing the accompaniment removal capability. Moreover, \acrshort{DDPM} have shown great generalization capabilities, making them a promising research line for the separation problem~\citep{araki-30years}. This may also benefit the computational analysis of Carnatic music in the long run.
    \item[\textbf{RO-5} $\rightarrow$]  \textbf{Develop a unified framework for the computational analysis of Carnatic music.} Standardized and community-driven code frameworks for specific research topics bring enormous benefits to the research community~\citep{raffel-mir_eval,bock-madmom,pariente-asteroid,steinmetz2020auraloss}. In this thesis we aim to provide access to models–-pre-trained if possible, tools, and datasets for the computational analysis of Carnatic music that can be used by researchers and practitioners interested on the topic. This should also be a framework that contributes to integrate \acrshort{DL} systems into Carnatic musicology studies, bridging current methodological gaps and promoting culturally-aware \acrshort{DL} applications in Carnatic music analysis.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch05_sep_nongen}} (covering \textbf{RO-1} and \textbf{RO-3}) delves deeply into the task of \acrshort{SVE} for Carnatic music. It first describes our work on vocal separation via gradual accompaniment removal applied on clean, multi-stem data for non-Carnatic recordings, showing that it provides refined separations over the baseline system. Subsequently, we present how this approach is extended to perform \acrshort{SVE} for Carnatic music by solely using data with source bleeding for training. The multi-step, gradual nature of the approach allows for further analysis and understanding of the gradual separation process, which enables us to discriminate the accompaniment from the singing voice even in the presence of source bleeding and other interferences in the ground-truth vocal stems.
%
This work includes the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A training strategy to perform iterative, gradual accompaniment removal from music mixtures in the waveform domain. The approach is applied to a lightweight system that shows competitive performance against waveform-based separation systems, validating also the potential of time-step conditioning to gradually separate music sources.
    \item \textbf{\textit{cold-diff-sep.}} A bleeding-aware \acrshort{SVE} system that achieves competitive removal of Carnatic-specific instruments over compared systems.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Chapter~\ref{ch06_sep_gen}} (covering \textbf{RO-1}, \textbf{RO-3} and \textbf{RO-4}) presents the research efforts toward generative-based \acrshort{SVE} using diffusion models. It first presents a waveform generation \acrshort{DDPM} which is trained to generate separated vocals only conditioned on the corresponding mixture. It then describes how, aiming at reducing the computational demands, we develop an alternative diffusion separation system that operates on an encoded and compact representation. The system generates a latent vector corresponding to the separated vocals, again only providing mixtures as reference. However, both systems are developed using clean multi-stem recordings. Building upon the conclusions from these works, we employ latent diffusion to perform generative separation of Carnatic music vocals assuming only access to Carnatic multi-stem data with source bleeding. We present how we condition and guide the system toward reducing the inherent bleeding in the openly available multi-stem collection for this repertoire.
%
The contributions related to this work are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\textit{diff-dmx:}} a waveform generation model based on \acrshort{DDPM} to perform singing voice separation from music mixtures that levels the deterministic baselines on interference removal and achieves competitive vocal quality.
    \item \textbf{\textit{ldm-dmx:}} a \acrshort{DDPM} operating on the latent space to provide an efficient and less data-hungry alternative to diff-dmx. It excels on interference removal and levels the non-generative baselines in spectral quality.
    \item \textbf{\textit{ldm-dmx-bd:}} a latent \acrshort{DDPM} that is pre-trained using multi-stem data with bleeding but further guided toward cleaner generations leveraging the flexibility and adaptability of the diffusion sampling process.
\end{itemize}
