Deep Neural Network (DNN) based audio processing models often fail to generalize from their test sets to real-world conditions, where performance can degrade substantially. This thesis pursues to reduce this gap by improving the ecological validity of the (often synthetic) data.
We develop this idea across three audio processing tasks. 

\begin{itemize}


\item (i) For binaural Speech Enhancement (SE) in Hearing Aids (HAs) we propose an evaluation methodology. Instead of relying on test signals under lab conditions, we propose to leverage spatial audio for generating realistic speech-in-noise  3D test scenes, which are played back thorugh a loudspeaker array and recorded with a KU100 dummy head, both wearing and not wearing HAs. The recordings are then used to compare the HA traditional algorithms with offline DNN-based models, showing that the latter generealize better.

\item (ii) For normal-hearing monaural speech enhancement, we examine three strategies for raising the ecological validity of synthetic Room Impulse Response (RIR) datasets — multiband absorption, source directivity, receiver directivity — and show that frequency-dependent absorption coefficients (MB-RIRs) yield measurable gains when using real RIRs in the evaluation. 

\item (iii) For music source separation (MSS) of live recordings, where the performance of studio-trained models degrades because they ignore venue acoustics, the PA system response, and audience noise, we introduce CrowdioSet (real ambience tracks plus synthetic sing-alongs, generated from zero-shot singing voice conversions) and PaRIRset (stereo impulse responses captured across 40 real professional concert venues), and show that training on them improves separation under live conditions both objectively and subjectively.

\end{itemize}
Across all three tasks, our results support enhancing the realism of supervised training data as an effective alternative to scaling models or architectural changes.
