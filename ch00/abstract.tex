\acrfull{DNN} based audio processing models often fail to generalize from their test sets to real-world conditions, where performance can degrade substantially. This thesis pursues to reduce this gap by improving the ecological validity of the (often synthetic) data.
We develop this idea across three audio processing tasks. 

\begin{itemize}


\item \textbf{\textit{(i)}} For binaural \acrfull{SE} in \acrfull{HAs} we propose an evaluation methodology. Instead of relying on test signals under lab conditions, we propose to leverage spatial audio for generating realistic speech-in-noise  3D test scenes, which are played back thorugh a loudspeaker array and recorded with a KU100 dummy head, both wearing and not wearing HAs. The recordings are then used to compare the HA traditional algorithms with offline DNN-based SE models, showing that the latter generealize better.

\item \textbf{\textit{(ii)}} For normal-hearing monaural speech enhancement, we examine three strategies for raising the ecological validity of synthetic \acrfull{RIR} datasets — multiband absorption, source directivity and receiver directivity — and show that frequency-dependent absorption coefficients yield measurable gains when using real RIRs in the evaluation. 

\item \textbf{\textit{(iii)}} For \acrfull{MSS} of live recordings from the audience, where the performance of studio-trained models degrades because of ignoring the venue acoustics, the speaker system response and audience noise, we introduce CrowdioSet (real ambience tracks plus synthetic sing-alongs, generated from zero-shot singing voice conversions) and PaRIRset (stereo impulse responses captured across 40 real professional concert venues), and show that training on them improves separation under live conditions, both objectively and subjectively.

\end{itemize}
In each of the three tasks, we obtain performance gains without adding complexity at inference, investing it upstream in building more ecologically valid data instead.
